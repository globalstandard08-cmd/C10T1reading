European Transport Systems

For much of the 20th century, European transport policy was focused almost entirely on building new roads and expanding the highway network. Governments believed that economic growth depended on making it easier and faster for people and goods to travel by car and truck. Massive investment went into motorways, bridges, and tunnels, while railways and public transport systems were often neglected or allowed to decline. By the 1990s, however, it became clear that this approach had created serious problems: severe traffic congestion, worsening air pollution, rising carbon emissions, and growing dependence on imported oil.

In response, the European Union developed a new strategy aimed at creating a sustainable and efficient transport system for the future. The core idea was to shift the balance between different modes of transport, moving freight and passengers away from roads and onto railways, inland waterways, and short-sea shipping. This policy, known as the ‘modal shift’, was designed to reduce environmental damage, improve safety, and make transport more reliable and affordable. The EU set specific targets: by 2030, 30% of road freight over 300 km should shift to other modes, and by 2050 this figure should rise to 50%.

Rail transport has been the biggest beneficiary of this change in policy. High-speed rail networks have expanded rapidly across the continent, connecting major cities and competing effectively with air travel for journeys of up to 600 km. New high-speed lines in France, Spain, Germany, and Italy have cut travel times significantly — for example, the journey from Paris to Marseille now takes just over three hours, compared to more than six hours before. Rail freight has also been modernised, with new cross-border services, improved terminals, and better interoperability between different national systems. Although rail still carries less than 20% of all freight in Europe, its market share is growing steadily.

Inland waterways, which carry around 6% of European freight, have also received renewed attention. Many rivers and canals were built centuries ago and had fallen into disrepair. Today, they are being upgraded and connected to form a comprehensive network that can handle larger barges and carry goods between countries efficiently. Water transport is particularly suitable for heavy or bulky goods such as coal, grain, and construction materials. It is also the most environmentally friendly option, producing far less carbon dioxide and noise than road or rail.

Urban transport has been another key area of reform. Cities across Europe have introduced measures to reduce car use and improve public transport, cycling, and walking. These include congestion charges in London and Stockholm, dedicated bus and tram lanes, extensive cycle networks in Amsterdam and Copenhagen, and pedestrian zones in historic city centres. Some cities have also invested in metro systems and light rail to provide fast, reliable services. The aim is not just to reduce pollution and congestion, but also to improve quality of life, make cities more attractive places to live and work, and encourage healthier lifestyles.

Despite these successes, major challenges remain. Transport demand is still growing, driven by population increase, economic growth, and global trade. Many rural areas still have poor transport links, and public transport services are often expensive or infrequent. Cross-border travel can still be complicated by different technical standards, regulations, and language barriers. Furthermore, while transport has become more efficient, it still accounts for around 25% of the EU’s total greenhouse gas emissions, and progress towards climate targets has been slower than expected.

Looking ahead, the focus is now on digitalisation and new technologies. Intelligent transport systems use data, sensors, and automation to manage traffic flow, improve safety, and reduce delays. Electric vehicles, hydrogen-powered trains, and alternative fuels are being developed to cut emissions further. The ultimate goal is a fully integrated European transport network, where passengers and goods can move seamlessly between different countries and different modes of transport, with minimal impact on the environment.
